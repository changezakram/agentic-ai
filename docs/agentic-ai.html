Agentic AI

1 Introduction

Artificial Intelligence (AI) is evolving rapidly, and one of the most exciting developments is the emergence of Agentic AI. Unlike traditional AI systems that simply respond to input, Agentic AI systems can reason, plan, act, and adapt autonomously. This blog post will walk you through the basics of Agentic AI‚Äîwhat it is, how it works, how it differs from other AI approaches, and why it matters.

1.1 What Is Agentic AI?

Agentic AI refers to intelligent systems that act with intent, autonomy, and adaptability to achieve defined goals. Unlike traditional AI, these agents not only respond to commands but also plan, decide, and act proactively ‚Äî often without step-by-step human direction.

They can perceive their environment, break down complex objectives into manageable tasks, interact with tools and APIs, and adapt their behavior based on feedback and context. In essence, they function as proactive collaborators, not just reactive assistants.

Google Cloud defines agentic AI as systems with memory, planning, and tool-use capabilities, operating in dynamic feedback loops. IBM and McKinsey emphasize their role as organizational actors ‚Äî capable of taking initiative, making decisions aligned with goals, and integrating seamlessly with human teams.

‚ÄúAgentic AI systems are dynamic, goal-seeking entities that can plan and act autonomously.‚Äù ‚Äî IBM

1.2 How It Differs From Traditional and Generative AI

AI Type Behavior Limitation
Traditional AI Rule-based or ML-based automation Rigid, task-specific
Generative AI Generates text, images, audio, or video using LLMs, diffusion, GANs, etc. Reactive: one prompt ‚Üí one response
Agentic AI Goal-oriented planning and action Proactive, multi-step autonomy

IBM describes the progression clearly: generative AI creates content across modalities, while agentic AI follows through on tasks by interpreting ambiguous instructions, planning multi-step workflows, and taking initiative.

McKinsey further notes that agentic systems assume decision-making responsibilities by incorporating context, risk, and organizational goals into their actions.

1.4 How Agentic AI Works

Agentic AI is made possible by combining several key components:

  1. Reasoning Engine (usually an LLM)
  2. Planner (to decompose goals)
  3. Memory (to retain context)
  4. Tool Use (to interact with external systems)
  5. Feedback Loop (to adapt in real time)

Google calls this an architecture of ‚Äúreasoning, memory, tools, and planning.‚Äù IBM adds that these systems are typically built with a modular wrapper around a core LLM, which provides autonomy and coordination capabilities.

McKinsey takes this further by introducing a Decision-Making Maturity Model, showing how agents evolve from assistive to autonomous roles.

1.6 Key Risks

With great autonomy comes greater responsibility‚Äîand risk.

  • Unintended Autonomy: Agents may act without proper oversight
  • Bias and Hallucinations: Decisions may be based on flawed or biased reasoning
  • Privacy Concerns: Tool-using agents must handle sensitive data with care
  • Brand Consistency: In customer contexts, inappropriate tone or overpersonalization can damage trust

IBM warns that agentic systems magnify the impact of AI errors:
> ‚ÄúA flawed generative response might misinform‚Äîa flawed agentic response might act on that misinformation.‚Äù

1.7 Risk Management Strategies

McKinsey, IBM, and Google recommend strategies to mitigate risks:

  • Bounded Autonomy: Define task scope and tool permissions
  • Human-in-the-loop: Require approvals for high-stakes decisions
  • Audit Trails: Log all agent actions for transparency and review
  • Brand & Tone Governance: Use tone filters for customer-facing agents
  • Scenario Testing: Evaluate agent behavior in simulated environments
  • Collaborative Governance: Assign oversight responsibilities to cross-functional teams

1.8 Best Practices for Implementation

From the Agentic Artificial Intelligence book: - Start with pilot programs in controlled environments - Use hybrid human‚ÄìAI workflows to maintain oversight while leveraging automation - Scale gradually while building internal support through demonstrated success - Encourage agents and employees to learn from each other for continuous improvement

1.9 Broader Implications

Agentic AI is more than just a new technology‚Äîit‚Äôs a paradigm shift:

  • From task execution to task ownership
  • From tools to teammates
  • From command-response to goal-driven collaboration

This will reshape work in both structure and culture. As McKinsey notes, organizations must evolve to become agentic themselves: more decentralized, flexible, and responsive.

‚ÄúAgentic AI won‚Äôt just change how work is done‚Äîit will change what work means.‚Äù
